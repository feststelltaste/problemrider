## How to Apply ◆

> Legacy system knowledge often resides with isolated individuals. User communities create peer networks where users help each other, share best practices, and collectively advocate for improvements.

- Create an online forum, discussion board, or channel where users can ask questions, share tips, and discuss workflows. Use existing platforms like Microsoft Teams, Slack, or a simple web forum rather than building from scratch.
- Seed the community with initial content: post answers to common questions, share workflow tips, and invite experienced users to contribute their expertise.
- Designate community moderators who can answer questions, connect users with relevant resources, and escalate genuine bugs or feature requests to the development team.
- Host regular community events such as monthly office hours, webinars on new features, or Q&A sessions with the development team. These events build engagement and create a feedback channel.
- Monitor community discussions to identify recurring pain points, feature requests, and usability issues that should inform the development roadmap.
- Recognize and reward active community members who help others, creating incentives for knowledge sharing and reducing the burden on formal support channels.

## Tradeoffs ⇄

> User communities scale support and knowledge sharing organically but require investment in facilitation and moderation to remain healthy.

**Benefits:**

- Scales support beyond what a dedicated support team can handle, as experienced users answer questions from their peers.
- Captures and shares tacit knowledge about workflows, workarounds, and best practices that would otherwise remain in individual users' heads.
- Creates a continuous feedback loop between users and the development team, surfacing real-world usage patterns and pain points.
- Reduces user isolation by connecting people who share the same challenges, building a sense of community around the system.

**Costs and Risks:**

- An unmoderated community can become a complaint forum that amplifies negativity rather than solving problems.
- Communities require sustained facilitation effort; an abandoned community with unanswered questions is worse than no community at all.
- Incorrect advice from community members can mislead other users, requiring moderation to flag or correct inaccurate information.
- The development team must be prepared to act on community feedback, or users will lose trust in the channel.

## How It Could Be

> Legacy systems often have small groups of power users who have developed deep expertise but have no way to share it broadly.

A legacy enterprise planning system is used by over five hundred planners across a large retail organization. Each regional office has one or two power users who understand the system deeply, but their knowledge is shared only with immediate colleagues through informal conversations. The organization creates a dedicated Teams channel for system users, seeding it with FAQs and a "Tip of the Week" series contributed by power users from different regions. Within three months, the channel has become an active community where planners share custom report templates, discuss workflow optimizations, and help each other troubleshoot issues. The IT support team notices a decline in routine support tickets because many questions are answered by peers in the community before they reach the help desk. The development team uses community discussions to identify the five most-requested improvements for the next release cycle.
