## How to Apply ◆

> Legacy systems are complex and often lack self-service help resources. Trained personal support provides a safety net for users who cannot resolve issues on their own.

- Establish a dedicated support team with deep knowledge of the legacy system, including its known issues, workarounds, and common user mistakes. Generic IT help desk staff without system-specific training cannot provide effective support.
- Create tiered support levels: first-line support handles common questions using documented solutions, while escalation paths lead to specialists for complex issues that require system expertise.
- Train support staff on the most common user workflows and the most frequent problems users encounter. Provide them with a searchable internal knowledge base so they can quickly find and share solutions.
- Implement a ticketing system that tracks all support interactions, creating a data source for identifying recurring problems that should be fixed in the system rather than handled repeatedly by support.
- Offer multiple support channels appropriate to the user base: phone and email for users who are less technically comfortable, chat for quick questions, and screen sharing for complex issues that are hard to describe verbally.
- Collect user feedback after each support interaction to measure satisfaction and identify areas where support quality can be improved.

## Tradeoffs ⇄

> Personal support provides the most effective user assistance but is the most expensive to scale and maintain.

**Benefits:**

- Provides immediate, context-specific help that self-service resources cannot match, especially for complex or unusual problems in legacy systems.
- Bridges the communication gap between users and the development team by surfacing recurring issues and user needs.
- Reduces user frustration by providing a reliable escalation path when self-service resources are insufficient.
- Generates valuable data about system problems, usability issues, and feature gaps through support ticket analysis.

**Costs and Risks:**

- Personal support is expensive to staff and scale, especially for legacy systems with large user bases spread across time zones.
- Over-reliance on personal support can mask fundamental usability problems that should be fixed in the system itself.
- Support staff turnover creates knowledge loss, requiring continuous training investment and thorough documentation.
- Users who receive inconsistent or incorrect advice from support lose trust in both the support team and the system.

## Examples

> For legacy systems with steep learning curves and limited documentation, personal support is often the only effective way to help users through complex workflows.

A legacy regulatory compliance system is used by compliance officers who are domain experts but not technology specialists. The system has dozens of complex workflows for filing reports with different regulatory bodies, each with different requirements and deadlines. After a major system update changes several workflows, the support team is overwhelmed with questions. The organization invests in training two dedicated compliance system specialists who understand both the regulatory domain and the system's technical details. These specialists provide personalized support through screen-sharing sessions, walk users through changed workflows, and document the most common questions as knowledge base articles. Support escalations to the development team decrease because the specialists can resolve most issues independently, and compliance officers report feeling supported rather than abandoned during the transition. The support ticket data also reveals three workflow changes that confused nearly every user, prompting targeted interface improvements.
