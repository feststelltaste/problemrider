## How to Apply ◆

> Legacy systems with complex workflows are difficult to learn from written documentation alone. Video tutorials provide visual, step-by-step demonstrations that users can follow at their own pace.

- Identify the highest-impact topics for video tutorials by analyzing support tickets, onboarding feedback, and user community questions. Focus on workflows that are difficult to explain in text.
- Keep videos short and focused: three to five minutes per video, covering a single task or concept. Long, comprehensive videos are rarely watched to completion.
- Record screen-capture videos with clear narration that explains not just what to click but why each step is performed and what the expected outcome is.
- Organize videos into a searchable library categorized by topic, workflow, and user role. Provide a table of contents with timestamps for longer videos.
- Include captions and transcripts for accessibility and for users who prefer reading or who work in environments where audio is impractical.
- Establish a maintenance process to update or re-record videos when the interface changes. Outdated videos that show a different interface than what users see are confusing and undermine trust in the tutorial library.

## Tradeoffs ⇄

> Video tutorials are the most effective medium for demonstrating complex visual workflows but are expensive to produce and maintain.

**Benefits:**

- Provides a visual learning experience that is significantly more effective than text documentation for interface-oriented tasks.
- Enables self-paced learning where users can pause, rewind, and rewatch as needed without imposing on colleagues' time.
- Reduces onboarding time by providing consistent, high-quality training that does not depend on the availability of experienced colleagues.
- Decreases support ticket volume for "how do I" questions by providing visual answers that users can find independently.

**Costs and Risks:**

- Video production requires time, equipment, and presentation skills that not all development teams possess.
- Videos become outdated quickly when the interface changes, and outdated videos are worse than no videos because they show incorrect steps.
- Videos are not searchable in the same way text is, making it harder for users to find the specific information they need within a video.
- Hosting and streaming video content requires infrastructure and bandwidth that may not be available in all deployment environments.

## How It Could Be

> Visual learners and users in time-pressured environments benefit enormously from video tutorials that show rather than tell.

A legacy financial reporting system has a complex month-end closing process that involves running calculations, reviewing exception reports, making adjustments, and submitting final figures. New finance staff receive a fifty-page procedure manual and typically require two or three month-end cycles with mentoring before they can perform the process independently. The team creates a series of eight short videos, each covering one step of the closing process, showing exactly which screens to navigate, which buttons to click, and which values to verify. New staff watch the relevant video before performing each step for the first time and refer back to specific videos when unsure. The mentoring period decreases from three months to one, and existing staff use the videos as refreshers for steps they perform infrequently, reducing errors in the less-common parts of the closing process.
